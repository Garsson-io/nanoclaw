# Horizon: Human-Agent Interface

*"The system's value is zero if its stakeholders can't tell whether it did the right thing."*

## Problem

The product vision is non-technical vertical experts giving work tickets and the system handling the rest. But today, the system's output is technical: PRs, diffs, CI results, kaizen issues. A printing workshop manager can't evaluate whether a PR is correct. They can approve it (rubber-stamp) or ignore it (no oversight). Neither is acceptable.

Without a human-agent interface:
- **Rubber-stamp approval** — stakeholders approve everything because they can't evaluate, quality collapses silently
- **Review bottleneck** — stakeholders refuse to approve because they don't understand, throughput collapses
- **No trust calibration** — the system doesn't know whether its stakeholders are actually reviewing or just clicking "approve"
- **Communication is noisy** — stakeholders get Telegram notifications for every merge but can't distinguish routine from important

## Taxonomy

| Level | Name | What the stakeholder experiences | Mechanism |
|-------|------|----------------------------------|-----------|
| **L0** | Raw output | PRs and diffs. Non-technical stakeholders can't evaluate. | GitHub/Telegram notifications |
| **L1** | Summarized output | Plain-language summary of what changed and why, for the stakeholder. | Case summary in messages, stakeholder-facing PR descriptions |
| **L2** | Impact-oriented | Changes in business terms. "Customers can now filter by date." | Domain-aware summarization |
| **L3** | Structured approval | Routine changes auto-merge. Novel changes surface with decision framing. | Risk classification, approval tiers |
| **L4** | Trust calibration | Track which approvals correlate with good outcomes vs rubber-stamps. | Approval outcome correlation |
| **L5** | Proactive communication | System knows when to notify and when to stay quiet. | Smart notification routing |
| **L6** | Collaborative reasoning | Structured dialogue for complex decisions. Options in domain terms. Reasoning captured and reused. | Decision-support framework |

## You Are Here

**L0.** Telegram merge notification with technical PR title ("fix: guard grep in pr-policy test-exceptions parser"). Agent-generated case summaries in conversation (unstructured, not stakeholder-facing). PR descriptions are developer-facing.

## What Exists

| Component | Level | Location |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| Telegram merge notification | L0 | `kaizen-reflect.sh` (send_telegram_ipc) |
| Case summaries in messages | L0 | Agent-generated, unstructured |

## L0→L1: Plain-Language Summaries (next step)

**Problem L1 solves:** Vertical expert gets "PR merged: fix: guard grep in pr-policy test-exceptions parser." They don't know what this means. L1: plain-language summary: "Fixed a bug where the CI system would crash when checking test coverage on PRs with no test exceptions."

**Rough shape:** Post-merge notification includes a stakeholder-facing summary (generated by the agent, checked for jargon). Summary is written for the specific vertical's audience (printing workshop vs insurance). The agent includes a `stakeholder_summary` field in its case completion, which the notification pipeline uses.

**Signal to escalate to L2:** Stakeholders consistently ask "but what does this mean for me?" — summaries are accurate but still too technical, not connected to business impact.

## L2–L3: Visible but not designed

**L2 (impact-oriented):** Problem: "Fixed authentication middleware" tells a stakeholder nothing about business impact. Need: domain-aware summarization that maps code changes to business outcomes. "Customers who were locked out after password reset can now log in immediately." Open question: who provides the domain mapping — the vertical config? The agent's understanding of the business? A domain glossary?

**L3 (structured approval):** Problem: every PR needs human approval, regardless of risk. Routine changes (doc updates, formatting, test-only changes) don't need human judgment. Need: risk classification. Low-risk → auto-merge. High-risk → structured decision-support for the stakeholder: "This change modifies how customer data is stored. Here are the options we considered and why we chose this one. Do you approve?" Open question: who defines risk tiers — the harness or the vertical?

## L4–L6: Horizon

**L4 (trust calibration):** Track whether stakeholder approvals are genuine reviews or rubber-stamps (e.g., approval within 10 seconds of notification = likely rubber-stamp). Use this to adjust what surfaces for review — if a stakeholder rubber-stamps everything, escalate more rather than less.

**L5 (proactive communication):** The system identifies when stakeholders should know something unprompted (architectural decisions, tradeoffs, risks) and when it should NOT communicate (routine changes that would be noise). Signal-to-noise optimization.

**L6 (collaborative reasoning):** For complex decisions, the system engages the stakeholder in a structured dialogue: presents options with tradeoffs in domain terms, asks for their reasoning, captures it, and uses it to improve future autonomous decisions. The stakeholder becomes a teacher, not just an approver.

## What We Can't See Yet

Beyond L6, the human-agent interface becomes a genuine partnership: the system proactively identifies business opportunities the stakeholder hasn't thought of, based on patterns it sees across cases and data. This is where the system starts creating value, not just executing instructions. That territory is closer to AGI than to current capabilities.

## Relationship to Other Horizons

- **HAI gates Autonomous Kaizen L7** — auto-merge without stakeholder understanding is dangerous; trust must be earned through L1-L4 before L7 is safe
- **HAI is THE product interface** — for non-technical vertical experts, this horizon IS the system
- **Observability feeds HAI L4+** — trust calibration requires tracking approval quality and outcomes
- **Cost Governance interacts with HAI L3** — budget delegation is a trust decision
